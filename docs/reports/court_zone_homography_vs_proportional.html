Court zone mapping · method comparison

Homography vs. proportional grid for the 9-zone court map

Tested against both ground-truth videos in ground_truth_template/. Built and validated by re-running this repo's own pipeline — not a fresh design, since the homography machinery already existed in court_detector.py, just switched off.

Verdict
Use homography, layered over the proportional grid as a fallback — not either method alone. Homography alone is the more geometrically correct formula and measurably closer to ground truth when it succeeds, but on 8–50% of shots (depending on the video) the detected court quadrilateral doesn't cover the point and it returns nothing. The hybrid (CourtCalibration.zone_for(), already written, currently switched off) gets homography's accuracy gain with the proportional grid's 100% coverage as a safety net.

1. What each method actually is

Both proposals end at the same final step — slicing a half-court into 3×3 bands via zone_grid.zone_number(), already in the codebase and confirmed to match both hand-drawn ground-truth grids exactly (back/mid/front rows, mirrored columns on the far half). They differ only in what coordinate space feeds that formula:

  1. Proportional grid (shipped today). Takes the pixel bounding box of where players were observed standing (5th–95th percentile, court_calibration.py), and divides that box into thirds directly in image-pixel space.
  2. Homography / bird's-eye. Detects the real court boundary (green-surface contour) in several frames, takes the median quadrilateral, and computes a perspective transform to a true 610×1340 cm rectangle — the actual physical badminton court. Any pixel maps to real court-plane centimeters, and that gets sliced into thirds.

Neither hardcodes anything per-video: the proportional grid's box comes from that video's own observed player positions; homography's constants (610×1340 cm) are the physical dimensions of a badminton court, not a tuned per-video ratio, and the corners are detected fresh from each frame.

2. Visual check: does the projected grid match the hand-drawn ground truth?

For each video, court_detector.detect_court_corners() + compute_homography() (both already in the repo) were run on an in-play frame, then the equal-thirds grid was projected back into image space via the inverse homography and overlaid.

Video 2 — Badminton_video_example_2.mp4

On in-play frames the automatically generated trapezoid tracks the true sidelines, centerline, and net almost exactly — on both videos, despite different camera framing, resolution, and broadcast style. This alone is hard for the proportional grid to match: it divides a pixel-space box into equal thirds, which can't reproduce the way perspective compresses the far side of the court.

Failure mode, also checked: on frames with no full court in view — intro walkouts, replays, celebration close-ups — corner detection correctly returns nothing rather than a bad grid. This is why calibrate_homography() already restricts sampling to rally windows and takes a median across many samples instead of trusting one frame.

3. Quantitative check: real shuttle positions against the ground-truth CSV

Re-ran video 1 through the full pipeline (rally segmentation → shot detection → real TrackNetV3 shuttle positions, all unchanged) and, using the exact same shuttle pixel position per shot, scored three ways against Badminton_video_example_ground_truth.csv (53 shots matched by rally + position-in-rally):

Lower divergence is better — it's the gap between predicted and true back/mid/front (row) and left/center/right (column) shot-share distributions. Exact/exact-or-adjacent move within noise at this sample size (n=53); divergence is the more stable signal here.

Homography alone roughly halves both row and column divergence relative to the shipped method — it's a real, substantial improvement in matching the true spatial spread of shots, not a rounding difference. The hybrid keeps most of that gain (col divergence 0.226→0.189) while never leaving a shot unclassified.

This is the honest caveat: on video 1, homography alone covered 85% of shots; on video 2, only 50.6%. Different broadcast framing and lighting make corner detection noisier for some videos, and this is exactly the generalization risk the "no hardcoding" requirement is worried about. A pure homography-only rollout would silently lose over half the video 2 shots to "no zone" — the hybrid's fallback is what makes the method actually safe to ship across videos this project hasn't seen yet.

Recommendation

Turn on what's already built: pass use_homography=True to calibrate_from_video() in main.py's _detect_shots() (currently defaults to False, a decision made in Phase C.1 before real shuttle tracking existed — back when this coordinate system was being fed noisy player-foot-position proxies, not actual shuttle contact points). CourtCalibration.zone_for() already implements the hybrid fallback exactly as tested above, so this is a one-line flip plus re-validation against the ground-truth CSV, not new engineering.
